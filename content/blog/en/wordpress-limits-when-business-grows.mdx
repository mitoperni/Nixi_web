There's a pattern we see repeatedly: someone starts with WordPress, the site works, the business grows, and at some point the website starts being a problem instead of a solution. Forms that don't scale, impossible integrations, the site getting slower as you add content. This isn't bad luck: it's a structural limitation of WordPress.

## WordPress was designed for blogs, not complex businesses

WordPress was born in 2003 as a blogging platform. Its fundamental architecture hasn't changed: a MySQL database, PHP processing every request, and a plugin system to add functionality.

This works well for simple informational websites. But when your business needs more, problems start.

## The real limits you'll encounter

### 1. Performance under load

A standard WordPress site makes between 30 and 80 database queries for each page that loads. With few simultaneous visits this isn't a problem. But if your site appears in the press, you launch a campaign, or the business simply grows, the server gets saturated.

The typical solution is to add cache plugins, a CDN, and increase the hosting plan. Each solution adds cost and complexity. And even then, Next.js generates static HTML that serves without database queries.

### 2. Integrations with external systems

Do you need to connect your website with a CRM like HubSpot or Salesforce? With an ERP? With a custom booking system? With your own API?

In WordPress, either there's a plugin that does it (with its own limitations and cost), or you have to hire a developer to build something custom but within WordPress's limitations. In many cases, what you want to do simply isn't possible natively.

In Next.js, any integration is possible: we connect directly with any API, database or external service.

### 3. Custom business logic

Do you need a product configurator? A dynamic quoting system? A custom calculator? A booking flow specific to your sector?

In WordPress, you'll depend on third-party plugins that may or may not do exactly what you need, and that cost between €50 and €500 per year each. If the plugin you need doesn't exist, deep customisation within WordPress is expensive and fragile.

### 4. Dependency on plugins that disappear

WordPress plugins are developed by third parties. In 2025, 52% of plugin developers don't patch vulnerabilities. And some simply abandon their plugins.

If a plugin that's critical to your site loses support, you have three options: continue with an outdated plugin (security risk), find a replacement and migrate (cost and time), or hire someone to maintain it (ongoing cost).

### 5. "Plugin hell": when plugins fight each other

With 15-30 active plugins, it's common for updating one to break another's functionality. Plugin conflicts are one of the most common causes of broken WordPress sites, and diagnosing them can take hours of technical work.

## When is the right time to switch?

There are some clear signs:

- You need functionality that no plugin does exactly as you want
- Your site takes more than 3 seconds to load on mobile
- You've had a security incident or are concerned about security
- You're paying more than €100/month in plugins and maintenance
- A technician has had to intervene to resolve plugin conflicts
- You want an integration with an external system that has no official plugin
