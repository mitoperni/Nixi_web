Imagine opening your website one day and it won't load. Or worse: it loads, but with content you didn't put there. Spam links, redirects to strange pages, or directly the "This site may be hacked" message in Google. This happens to hundreds of thousands of WordPress sites every year. And it can happen to yours.

## Real WordPress security figures (2025 data)

The data from the WordPress ecosystem in 2025 is concerning:

- **64,782 known vulnerabilities** recorded in total in the WordPress ecosystem
- **250+ new vulnerabilities** discovered every week in plugins alone
- **36 new vulnerabilities per day** on average
- **43% of vulnerabilities are exploitable without authentication**, meaning anyone can exploit them without needing passwords
- **92% of successful hacks** come from plugins and themes, not the WordPress core
- **52% of plugin developers** didn't patch vulnerabilities before they were publicly disclosed
- More than **500,000 compromised websites** in 2025 alone

And the trend is upward: in 2026, vulnerability volume is expected to reach 55,000 in new CVEs alone.

## Why WordPress is such a big target

WordPress powers **42.8% of all websites worldwide** as of February 2026. This makes it the most profitable target for hackers: if you find an exploit in a popular plugin installed on 3 million sites, you can compromise 3 million sites at once.

Attacks are not personal. They're automated. Bots scanning the internet 24/7 looking for vulnerable versions of popular plugins like WooCommerce, Contact Form 7, Yoast SEO, Elementor... If you have an outdated version, it's a matter of hours.

## The security maintenance trap cycle in WordPress

The problem isn't that WordPress is inherently insecure. The problem is that keeping it secure requires:

1. Updating the WordPress core every time a new version is released
2. Updating every installed plugin (you have 15-30, right?)
3. Updating the theme
4. Verifying that no update broke anything
5. Monitoring for unauthorised access attempts
6. Having working daily backups

If you do all this well, you spend time or money. If you don't, you run a real risk.

## What happens if you get hacked?

- **Loss of Google ranking:** Google penalises and warns about compromised sites, which can make you disappear from results for weeks or months
- **Reputation damage:** Your customers see your site with spam or malware content
- **Compromised customer data:** If you have forms, an online store or user data, it may have been stolen
- **Cleaning cost:** Between €150 and €500 for a professional cleaning service, with no guarantee of success if the vulnerability isn't identified

## How security works in custom development

A website built with Next.js has no third-party plugins. It doesn't have a software core shared with 540 million websites. It has exactly the code it needs, reviewed and controlled.

The attack vector is minimal. No database exposed to the public. No admin panel at /wp-admin that bots know by heart. No plugins with vulnerabilities discovered every week.

This doesn't mean a custom website is invulnerable, but the attack surface is orders of magnitude smaller.

Your website security is your online business security. A compromised website can cost you much more than what it cost to build it.
